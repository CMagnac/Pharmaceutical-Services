Cystitis is a common condition in women. This inflammation of the bladder must be treated promptly to prevent progression to pyelonephritis. Pharmacist-led cystitis management in community pharmacies was officially launched on June 18, 2024. Community pharmacists are now authorized to perform Rapid Diagnostic Orientation Tests (TROD) for cystitis.

Clinical Signs of Cystitis in Women

This article focuses exclusively on women aged 16 to 65 years. All other patients should be referred to a physician within 24 hours. Escherichia coli, an enterobacterium, is responsible for 60-80% of cystitis cases.

A simple preventive hygiene measure consists of wiping from front to back. Between 25% and 45% of cystitis episodes resolve spontaneously with adequate hydration, consisting of 1.5 to 2 liters of still water per day.

Suggestive signs of acute cystitis include afebrile dysuria (burning sensation during urination) associated with pollakiuria (frequent urination).

The pharmacist must check for the absence of emergency exclusion criteria:

  • Hyperthermia above 38°C or hypothermia below 36°C;
  • Symptoms suggestive of pyelonephritis;
  • Chills.

Acute cystitis may be classified as uncomplicated, at risk of complications, or recurrent. If the patient presents for at least the third episode within one year, she must be referred for a medical consultation within 24 hours.

Any management of a woman of childbearing age requires assessment of potential pregnancy.

Exclusion criteria for pharmacist-led management include :

  • Presence of gynecological symptoms (leucorrhea, vulvar or vaginal pruritus) ;
  • An episode of cystitis within the previous two weeks ;
  • Use of fluoroquinolones within the past three months ;
  • Presence of an implanted venous catheter ;
  • Structural abnormality of the urinary tract ;
  • Severe renal impairment ;
  • Ongoing antibiotic therapy ;
  • Immunosuppression.

Common Principles in the Management of Tonsillitis and Cystitis

If the clinical presentation suggests suspected cystitis, the pharmacist provides a sterile urine collection container.

Cystitis Rapid Diagnostic Test (TROD)

The urine sample is collected within the pharmacy. Strict procedures must be followed to minimize the risk of false negatives and false positives.

False negatives may result from recent antibiotic use or high-dose ascorbic acid intake. The sample container may be contaminated by other bacteria if proper hand hygiene is not observed.

Best practice recommendations include:

  • The longer urine remains in the bladder, the more reliable the sample.
  • Before urination, the patient should wash her hands and intimate area with soap and rinse thoroughly.
  • The patient should begin urinating into the toilet, stop briefly, then continue into the container without filling it completely.
  • Close the container securely.
  • Wash hands again.

Urine dipsticks detect the presence or absence of biological markers indicative of bacterial infection. Leukocytes indicate inflammation of the bladder wall, while nitrites suggest the presence of enterobacteria in the urine.

Treatment of Acute Cystitis

A positive TROD result authorizes community pharmacists to prescribe antibiotics.

First-line treatment: fosfomycin trometamol. More than 80% of the dose is excreted unchanged in the urine. The recommended dosage is a single 3-gram dose.

Second-line treatment: pivmecillinam, a prodrug of mecillinam. The recommended dosage is 400 mg twice daily for three days. Before prescribing pivmecillinam, the pharmacist must ensure the absence of penicillin or cephalosporin allergy.

Fosfomycin trometamol should preferably be taken on an empty stomach, at bedtime, after bladder emptying. Pivmecillinam increases the effects and hematological toxicity of methotrexate.

French community pharmacists play an active role in prevention and public health initiatives. The ability to perform cystitis rapid diagnostic testing significantly reduces antibiotic misuse and antimicrobial resistance. Pharmacist-led cystitis management in community pharmacies represents a major step forward in expanding the professional scope of pharmacists.
