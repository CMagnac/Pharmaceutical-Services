Acute tonsillitis primarily affects children between 4 and 15 years of age. This infectious inflammation of the tonsils requires prompt management to prevent invasive Group A beta-hemolytic streptococcal infection (iGAS). Since October 31, 2023, community pharmacists in France are authorized to perform a Rapid Diagnostic Orientation Test (TROD) and prescribe antibiotics.

Clinical Signs of Acute Tonsillitis

Group A beta-hemolytic streptococcus (GAS or Streptococcus pyogenes) is responsible for 25-45% of cases in adults and 10-25% in children. However, 50-90% of tonsillitis cases are of viral origin.

There are five types of acute tonsillitis:

  • Pseudomembranous (infectious mononucleosis, diphtheria);
  • Erythematous with exudate (white tonsillitis);
  • Ulcerative-necrotic (Vincent's angina);
  • Vesicular (herpetic, herpangina);
  • Erythematous (red tonsillitis).

The main complication of acute tonsillitis is a peritonsillar abscess (quinsy). This is a collection of pus located between the tonsil and the pharyngeal muscle wall. It is associated with trismus (difficulty opening the mouth), and the pain is typically unilateral.

This article focuses exclusively on patients presenting without a medical prescription. Children under 10 years of age and pregnant women should be referred to a physician within 24 hours.

Suggestive signs of acute tonsillitis include sudden onset sore throat, odynophagia (painful swallowing), and red, swollen tonsils, sometimes accompanied by fever between 38°C and 39°C. In most cases, GAS tonsillitis resolves spontaneously within one week.

The pharmacist must check for emergency exclusion criteria:

  • Severe gastrointestinal symptoms (vomiting, diarrhea) in children;
  • Fever above 39.5°C.

Exclusion criteria for pharmacist-led management include:

  • Contraindication to recommended antibiotics (penicillins, cephalosporins, macrolides);
  • Recent return (within 2-5 days) from a tropical country with outdated diphtheria vaccination (risk of diphtheria);
  • Third consultation for odynophagia within six months;
  • A similar episode within the previous 15 days suggesting antibiotic resistance;
  • Skin rash in a child under 15 years (risk of scarlet fever);
  • Presence of an implanted venous catheter;
  • Severe renal impairment;
  • Ongoing antibiotic therapy;
  • Immunosuppression.

Some external local signs require medical referral:

  • Neck pain or torticollis limiting head movement;
  • Trismus;
  • Breathing or speech difficulties;
  • Hypersalivation.

NSAIDs should not be dispensed for sore throat, as they are frequently implicated in the development of peritonsillar abscesses. Symptoms should improve within 48 hours; otherwise, the patient must consult a physician.

If the patient is 15 years old or younger, the pharmacist performs a TROD. Otherwise, the McIsaac score is calculated.

Clinical Signs Points
Tonsillar swelling with exudate +1
Tender cervical lymphadenopathy +1
Temperature above 38°C +1
Age over 45 years -1
Absence of cough +1
McIsaac Score

If the McIsaac score is greater than 2, a TROD must be performed.

Treatment of Bacterial Tonsillitis

Amoxicillin

First-line treatment due to its lower risk of promoting bacterial resistance.

Adult Child
2 g/day in two divided doses, with or without food 50 mg/kg/day in two divided doses, with or without food
For six days For six days
Amoxicillin dosage in bacterial tonsillitis

Drug interactions with amoxicillin:

  • Methotrexate: increased risk of hematological toxicity (select an alternative antibiotic);
  • Allopurinol: increased risk of skin reactions (temporarily discontinue allopurinol).

When prescribing antibiotics for a patient receiving vitamin K antagonists, the pharmacist should advise closer INR monitoring.

Cephalosporins

Recommended in case of penicillin allergy.

Adult Child
200 mg/day in two divided doses with meals 8 mg/kg/day in two divided doses with meals
For five days For five days
Cefpodoxime dosage in bacterial tonsillitis

Cefuroxime (adults only): 500 mg/day in two divided doses after meals for four days.

Penicillin allergy is non-dose-dependent and unpredictable. As penicillins and cephalosporins are beta-lactams, cross-reactivity may occur. Any allergic reaction requires immediate discontinuation and urgent medical consultation.

Macrolides

Clarithromycin is recommended in case of beta-lactam allergy.

Adult Child
500 mg/day in two divided doses with meals 15 mg/kg/day in two divided doses with meals
For five days For five days
Clarithromycin dosage in bacterial tonsillitis

Drug interactions with clarithromycin are numerous. It acts as an enzyme inhibitor and prolongs the QT interval.

French community pharmacists play an active role in prevention and public health initiatives. The ability to perform rapid diagnostic tests for tonsillitis significantly reduces antibiotic misuse and antimicrobial resistance. Pharmacist-led management of acute tonsillitis represents a major step forward in expanding the professional scope of community pharmacists.
